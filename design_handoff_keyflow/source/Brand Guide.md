# KeyFlow Dashboard Design System & Brand Guide

## Overview

Design a modern, professional, client-facing dashboard for **KeyFlow**, Rain Design Labs' AI-first CRM and AI Employee control center for small businesses.

The dashboard is the front face of the KeyFlow system. It should feel clean, calm, premium, and easy for non-technical clients to understand, while still implying there is serious operational power under the hood.

KeyFlow is not trying to look like a traditional CRM packed with fields, tabs, and dense admin controls. It should feel more like an Apple-inspired command center for managing an AI employee: simple on the surface, capable underneath.

---

# Product Positioning

## What KeyFlow Is

KeyFlow is the dashboard where a client can see, guide, and approve the work their AI employee is doing.

It connects:

- Client business profile
- AI pilot setup
- Workflow maps
- AI training materials
- Lead and customer activity
- Voice call logs
- Email and SMS follow-up
- Human approvals
- Escalation rules
- Performance outcomes

## What KeyFlow Is Not

KeyFlow should not feel like:

- A generic CRM clone
- A developer admin panel
- A cluttered analytics platform
- A database viewer
- A heavy enterprise tool
- A chatbot interface with charts glued on

The interface should communicate:

> “Your AI employee is working. Here is what it knows, what it did, what needs your approval, and what results it produced.”

# Target Users

## Primary User

Small business owner or operator who is not technical.

Examples:

- Real estate agent
- Local service business owner
- Solo professional
- Small team manager
- Admin-heavy local business

## User Mindset

The user wants:

- Confidence
- Clarity
- Control
- Proof that work is happening
- Fast understanding without training
- Low stress
- No technical jargon

The user may be nervous about AI. The dashboard should reduce that anxiety by showing clear guardrails, human approval points, and understandable activity summaries.

# Design Principles

## Core Goals

- Information should be easy to scan and understand.
- Every screen should feel like part of the same product.
- Prioritize usability over decoration.
- Use whitespace generously.
- Reduce cognitive load.
- Maintain consistency across all pages and components.
- Make AI activity feel transparent and controllable.
- Show summaries first, then allow drill-down.
- Make the system feel powerful without making it feel complicated.

## Desired Feel

- Apple Human Interface Guidelines inspired
- Premium but approachable
- Modern SaaS application
- Executive-friendly
- Clean and organized
- Fast and responsive
- Mobile-friendly
- Supports light and dark mode
- Calm, trustworthy, and practical

## Emotional Target

The user should feel:

- “I understand what is happening.”
- “I am still in control.”
- “This is polished enough to trust.”
- “This could actually save me time.”
- “This is more like hiring an assistant than buying software.”

# Visual Style

## Overall Aesthetic

- Minimalist
- Soft rounded corners
- Subtle shadows
- Clean typography
- Neutral color palette
- Limited use of accent colors
- No excessive gradients
- No flashy animations
- No clutter
- No loud AI/neon styling
- No cyberpunk look
- No overly technical dashboard aesthetic

## Layout Philosophy

- Use card-based layouts.
- Maintain strong visual hierarchy.
- Group related information together.
- Avoid overcrowding screens.
- Keep navigation simple and predictable.
- Use progressive disclosure: show the simple version first, reveal deeper controls only when needed.
- Make each page answer one clear question.

Examples:

- Home answers: “What is happening today?”
- Pilot answers: “What are we setting up for this client?”
- Activity answers: “What did the AI employee do?”
- Approvals answer: “What needs my decision?”
- Knowledge answers: “What does the AI know?”

# Typography

## Font Style

Use:

- Manrope
- SF Pro Display equivalent
- Modern sans-serif fallback

## Hierarchy

### Page Title

- Large and bold
- 28–36px desktop
- 24–28px mobile
- Weight: 700 or 800

### Section Heading

- Medium-large
- 18–24px
- Weight: 700

### Card Title

- Clear medium emphasis
- 15–18px
- Weight: 650 or 700

### Body Text

- Readable and simple
- 14–16px
- Weight: 400–500

### Labels

- Smaller and subtle
- 11–13px
- Weight: 600
- Often uppercase for section labels or metadata

## Tone of Text

The dashboard copy should be plain-language and client-friendly.

Use:

- “Needs approval”
- “AI followed up”
- “Call summary”
- “Escalated to you”
- “Ready to launch”
- “Waiting on client info”

Avoid:

- “LLM execution complete”
- “Vectorized knowledge object”
- “Webhook payload received”
- “Inference event”
- “Tenant-scoped entity”

Technical language can exist in admin/developer views, not in the default client-facing view.

## Typography Rules

- Limit typography styles.
- Avoid decorative fonts.
- Maintain consistent sizing.
- Use strong contrast for readability.
- Avoid all-caps except for small labels, badges, and metadata.
- Keep line length comfortable.

# Spacing System

Use consistent spacing throughout the application.

Base spacing scale:

- 4px
- 8px
- 12px
- 16px
- 24px
- 32px
- 48px
- 64px

Rules:

- Never crowd elements together.
- Use larger spacing between sections.
- Use smaller spacing within related groups.
- Dashboard cards should feel breathable.
- Avoid dense enterprise-table spacing unless the user intentionally opens a detailed admin view.

---

# Shape, Radius, and Elevation

## Border Radius

Use soft, Apple-inspired radius.

Recommended:

- Small controls: 10–12px
- Inputs: 12px
- Cards: 18–24px
- Large panels: 24–28px
- Pills: 999px

## Shadows

Use subtle shadows only.

Light mode:

- Cards: soft low-opacity shadow
- Floating panels: slightly stronger shadow
- Avoid heavy drop shadows

Dark mode:

- Use borders and subtle surface contrast more than shadows

## Borders

Use light borders to separate cards and panels.

- Light mode border: `#E4E6E8`
- Dark mode border: `#303844`

### Style

- Rounded corners
- Soft shadow
- Light border
- Comfortable padding
- Clear title

- Optional status badge

Use cards for:

- KPIs
- Reports
- Charts
- Summaries

- Pilot steps
- AI training materials
- Approval items
- Workflow stages

### Card Types

#### Summary Card

Used for high-level dashboard summaries.

Examples:

- New leads contacted
- Calls handled
- Follow-ups sent
- Items awaiting approval

#### Action Card

Used when the user needs to do something.

Examples:

- Approve message
- Review call summary
- Upload source document
- Confirm pilot scope

#### Knowledge Card

Used for AI training materials.

Examples:

- Buyer Intake Brief
- Seller Listing Brief
- Closing Timeline Brief
- Real Estate Vocabulary
- Escalation Rules

#### Insight Card

Used for AI-generated recommendations.

Examples:

- “Three leads asked about financing this week.”
- “Most missed calls happen after 5 PM.”
- “Consider adding an after-hours buyer intake flow.”

## Buttons

### Primary Button

Used for:

- Main actions
- Save
- Submit
- Approve
- Create
- Launch pilot

Style:

- Filled primary blue
- White text
- Rounded pill or 12–14px radius
- Strong but not oversized

### Secondary Button

Used for:

- Supporting actions
- Filters
- Optional workflows
- View details
- Download

Style:

- Neutral background or outline
- Dark text in light mode
- Light text in dark mode

### Destructive Button

Used only for true destructive actions.

Examples:

- Delete workflow
- Remove knowledge source
- Disable AI follow-up

Style:

- Red text or red fill depending on severity
- Always require confirmation if destructive

### Button Rules

- Consistent height
- Rounded corners
- Clear hover states
- Accessible contrast
- Button labels should be verbs
- Avoid vague labels like “Submit” when a specific action is possible

Preferred labels:

- Approve follow-up
- Review summary
- Launch pilot
- Add knowledge source
- View call
- Send to client

---

## Forms

### Style

- Clean inputs
- Generous spacing
- Clear labels
- Simple validation
- Minimal visual noise

### Rules

- Always show labels.
- Keep forms visually lightweight.
- Group related fields.
- Use helper text when the user may be unsure.
- Use large textareas for business descriptions, pain points, tone notes, and guardrails.
- Do not make users write inside tiny boxes.

### KeyFlow Form Examples

- Business snapshot form
- Client onboarding form
- AI comfort zone form
- Workflow setup form
- Knowledge source upload form
- Approval rule configuration form

---

## Tables

### Style

- Clean rows
- Minimal borders
- Strong readability
- Subtle row hover
- Clear empty states

### Usage

Use tables only when data comparison matters.

Examples:

- Call logs
- Lead list
- Activity history
- Approval history
- Knowledge source list

### Rules

- Sticky headers when useful
- Sortable columns
- Searchable when appropriate
- Responsive on smaller screens
- Default to summary cards before tables
- Use detail drawers for row expansion instead of overwhelming full-page tables

---

## Modals

### Style

- Simple and focused
- Clear title
- Minimal distractions
- One primary action
- One safe cancel action

### Rules

- Only use when necessary.
- Keep content concise.
- Avoid nesting modals.
- Use side panels or drawers for richer detail views.

---

## Drawers / Side Panels

Use drawers for drill-down details without leaving the current workflow.

Good uses:

- Call transcript detail
- Lead profile detail
- Approval review
- Knowledge brief preview
- Workflow stage configuration

Drawer structure:

- Header with title and status
- Summary block first
- Details below
- Actions fixed at bottom when approval is needed

---

## Badges

Use badges for state, not decoration.

Examples:

- Ready
- Draft
- Needs approval
- Live
- Paused
- Escalated
- Completed
- Waiting on client
- AI suggested

Badge style should be soft, rounded, and low-noise.

---

## Empty States

Empty states are important because many clients will start with no data.

Every empty state should include:

- Plain-language explanation
- What happens next
- One clear action

Example:

**No calls yet**  
Once your AI employee starts answering calls, summaries and transcripts will appear here.

Button: **Set up voice agent**

# Dashboard Standards

## KPI Section

Place key metrics at the top of dashboards.

KeyFlow-specific examples:

- Leads contacted
- Calls handled
- Follow-ups sent
- Appointments requested
- Items awaiting approval
- Escalations
- Qualified leads
- Response time

Rules:

- Use KPI cards.
- Limit to 4–6 KPIs per row.
- Prioritize the most important metrics.
- KPI cards should include a short label, large number, and optional trend/context.
- Avoid showing too many numbers at once.

---

## Charts

Use charts only when they improve understanding.

Preferred chart types:

- Line charts
- Bar charts
- Area charts

Avoid:

- Excessive chart variety
- Decorative visualizations
- 3D charts
- Confusing custom charts

Charts should answer practical questions:

- Are more leads being followed up with?
- Are response times improving?
- Are calls being handled successfully?
- Are approvals creating bottlenecks?

---

## Data Density

Rules:

- Keep screens scannable.
- Avoid overwhelming users.
- Show summaries first.
- Allow drill-down into details.
- Use tables only when needed.
- Hide advanced technical metadata by default.

KeyFlow should have two layers:

1. **Simple client layer:** plain-language summaries and obvious actions.
2. **Power layer:** detailed records, logs, integrations, and advanced workflow controls.

The simple layer should always be the default.

# AI-Specific UX Rules

## AI Transparency

When AI generates something, label it clearly but calmly.

Examples:

- AI draft
- AI summary
- AI suggestion
- AI handled this call
- AI recommends review

Do not over-brand every AI action with sparkle icons or gimmicks.

## Human Control

Always make it clear what the human can do:

- Approve
- Edit
- Reject
- Escalate
- Pause workflow
- Change rule
- View source

## Guardrails

For any workflow involving customers, show:

- What AI can do
- What AI cannot do
- When AI escalates
- Whether approval is required

## Confidence and Risk

If confidence scoring is used, show it in plain language.

Preferred:

- Low confidence: needs review
- Medium confidence: review suggested
- High confidence: ready

Avoid exposing raw model scores unless in advanced/admin mode.

# AI Generation Rules

When generating any KeyFlow screen:

1. Follow this design system exactly.
2. Do not invent new visual styles.
3. Do not introduce new spacing systems.
4. Do not introduce new typography systems.
5. Use consistent components across all pages.
6. Maintain Apple-inspired simplicity.
7. Prioritize readability and usability.
8. Minimize visual noise.
9. Keep interfaces professional and modern.
10. Ensure all screens feel like part of the same application.
11. Use Manrope as the primary font.
12. Use RDL cyan blue `#00B3EA` sparingly as the primary accent.
13. Keep the default client experience non-technical.
14. Show summaries before details.
15. Use progressive disclosure for advanced controls.
16. Make human approval and control obvious.
17. Do not make the dashboard feel like a generic CRM.
18. Do not use flashy AI visuals, neon gradients, or gimmicky sparkle effects.
19. Every page should answer one clear user question.
20. Every important AI action should be explainable in plain language.
